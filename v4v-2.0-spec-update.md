# V4V 2.0: A Specification for Web Boostagrams

## Abstract

V4V 2.0 is a specification for attaching structured donation metadata (sender, amount, message, source) to Lightning payments made on the open web. It builds on the boostagram concept introduced by Podcasting 2.0 but adapts it for the realities of LNURL-pay, the diversity of Lightning wallets, and the Nostr ecosystem.

Where NIP-57 zaps brought rich-metadata Lightning tipping to Nostr notes, V4V 2.0 brings the same primitive to the rest of the internet: any web page, any creator, any project — without requiring the recipient to run zap-aware infrastructure, and without requiring the donor to have a Nostr identity at all. It is best understood as the off-Nostr complement to zaps, not a competitor to them.

The core design uses a Nostr kind 30078 event as a public metadata sidecar, joined to the Lightning payment by the bolt11 invoice's `payment_hash`. This sidesteps the wallet-compatibility problems of keysend, the size limits of LNURL comment fields, and the recipient-side infrastructure requirements of NIP-57 zaps, while supporting first-class anonymous donations, working with any standard LNURL-pay endpoint, and requiring no custom backend on either side.

This document specifies the protocol and explains the design choices behind it.

## Background

A *boostagram* is a Lightning payment with rich attached metadata: who sent it, how much, why, and where they were when they sent it. Podcasting 2.0 popularized the term: listeners boost a podcast episode and the metadata reaches the podcaster's tooling along with the sats.

The Podcasting 2.0 boostagram protocol is built on Lightning *keysend* (BLIP-10). Keysend is a spontaneous-payment primitive: the sender's node sends an HTLC directly to the recipient's node pubkey, with no invoice, and the metadata is attached as TLV records (specifically TLV record 7629169) inside the HTLC payload. The recipient's node decodes the TLVs and the boost arrives complete.

That model works beautifully for the podcast use case, where listeners use podcast-aware apps (Fountain, Castamatic, Podverse, Breez) that all support keysend. But translating it to the broader web for tipping any creator, donating to any project, with any Lightning wallet, this strategy runs into constraints the podcast world only solved by adopting purpose-built sender wallets.

In parallel, NIP-57 brought rich-metadata Lightning tipping into the Nostr ecosystem in the form of *zaps*. Zaps are an elegant solution within their scope: they cryptographically bind a Nostr-signed comment to a bolt11 invoice via `description_hash`, and the recipient's LNURL server publishes a signed receipt event vouching for settlement. But zaps assume the entire interaction lives on Nostr — a Nostr-identified sender, a Nostr-aware recipient LNURL endpoint, and a target Nostr event being zapped. They do not extend cleanly to the rest of the internet, where senders may have no Nostr identity, recipients may use LNURL providers that do not implement NIP-57, and the thing being tipped is a web page rather than a note.

V4V 2.0 occupies the gap: bringing the boostagram experience to arbitrary web destinations, with arbitrary Lightning wallets, and with anonymity as a first-class option. Where zaps are the right primitive for tipping inside Nostr, V4V 2.0 is the right primitive for tipping the open web.

## The Problem

For a static-site web app like a creator's homepage, a project landing page, or a donation portal, four constraints define the design space:

**1. Sender wallet diversity.** Donors arrive with whatever Lightning wallet they have. The dominant wallets among the most active donor demographic include Phoenix, Wallet of Satoshi, Cash App, Strike, and most LNURL-only wallets, which do not support keysend. Adopting the Podcasting 2.0 model would mean turning away most donors. This is the deal-breaker for keysend in this context.

**2. Sender Nostr-identity diversity.** Donors arriving from the open web may not be Nostr users at all. A protocol that requires a Nostr signing identity to participate excludes a large fraction of the audience that web-published content reaches. Even Nostr-identified donors may want to tip without attaching their pubkey to the payment for privacy reasons.

**3. Recipient LNURL-endpoint variability.** Some recipients run their own LNURL endpoints (LNbits, custom servers). Many recipients use third-party Lightning addresses (`@getalby.com`, `@walletofsatoshi.com`, `@strike.me`, `@phoenixwallet.me`). A protocol that requires the recipient's LNURL endpoint to implement specific extensions excludes everyone whose chosen Lightning provider hasn't shipped that support.

**4. Static site constraint.** Many recipients don't have a backend at all. A specification that requires server-side invoice generation to embed metadata in a bolt11's `description_hash` ruins this. The protocol needs to work with client-only code talking to a third-party LNURL-pay endpoint.

The intersection of these constraints rules out the obvious approaches:

- **Keysend (Podcasting 2.0)** fails on (#1).
- **Custom backend with `description_hash` linking** fails on (#4), and partially on (#3) since it requires recipient-controlled LNURL.
- **NIP-57 Zaps** fail on (#2) and (#3). Zaps require the donor to publish a signed kind 9734 from a Nostr identity (no first-class anonymous mode, and no path for non-Nostr donors), and require the recipient's LNURL endpoint to implement NIP-57's `allowsNostr`/`nostrPubkey` flow and publish kind 9735 receipts on settlement. Zaps also presuppose a Nostr event to zap — they have no native mechanism for tipping a destination that isn't a Nostr note.

V4V 2.0 is the design that fits inside the intersection of all four constraints.

## The Insight

What's universal across every Lightning wallet that can pay an LNURL invoice? Two things:

1. **The bolt11 invoice's `payment_hash`.** It's a 32-byte cryptographic identifier of the payment. Every node that handles the HTLC sees it, and the recipient's node always records it on settlement, regardless of what intermediate forwarding occurred.

2. **Public Nostr relays.** Every web app that has a Nostr-native identity layer already has Nostr connectivity.

If we use the `payment_hash` as a join key between the Lightning side and the Nostr side, we can store the boost metadata as a public Nostr event keyed by the payment hash. The Lightning payment itself is unchanged, it goes through whatever LNURL flow the donor's wallet supports. The metadata lives on Nostr, free of Lightning's protocol limitations. A recipient can then build a bot to read the `payment_hash` off LND on settlement, query Nostr for the matching event, and get the structured boost data.

This is the V4V 2.0 design.

## Relationship to NIP-57 Zaps

V4V 2.0 is not a replacement for zaps. The two protocols are complementary, addressing different surfaces of the Lightning-plus-Nostr design space:

| Dimension | NIP-57 Zaps | V4V 2.0 |
|---|---|---|
| What's being tipped | A Nostr event (note, article, profile) | Any web destination (page, project, lightning address) |
| Donor identity required | Yes — donor signs kind 9734 with a Nostr key | No — burner key supported as first-class anonymous mode |
| Recipient LNURL must implement | NIP-57 (`allowsNostr`, `nostrPubkey`, kind 9735 publishing) | Standard LNURL-pay (LUD-06) only |
| Cryptographic binding of metadata to invoice | Strong (`description_hash` of kind 9734) | Weak by default (`payment_hash` join only); strong as opt-in (see Future Work) |
| Settlement attestation visible to third parties | Yes — kind 9735 signed by recipient's LNURL provider | Optional — recipient may publish their own kind 1 receipt as the reference impl does |
| Works for non-Nostr donors | No | Yes |
| Works behind any Lightning address | Only those with NIP-57-aware LNURL | Any |

**Where zaps win:** zaps are the right primitive when both ends of the interaction are Nostr-native and the target is a Nostr event. The cryptographic chain (sender's signed 9734 → invoice's description_hash → LNURL provider's signed 9735) gives third parties a verifiable record of the tip without relying on the recipient to publish anything themselves. For tipping inside Nostr clients, this is the better-engineered protocol.

**Where V4V 2.0 wins:** V4V 2.0 is the right primitive when the tip is happening on the open web rather than inside Nostr — when the target isn't a Nostr event, when the donor may not have a Nostr identity, when the recipient's Lightning address is on a provider that doesn't implement NIP-57, or when the donor genuinely needs anonymity. Zaps cannot be made to fit any of these cases without breaking their own assumptions.

**On anonymity specifically:** NIP-57 mechanically permits a donor to sign a kind 9734 with an ephemeral key, but this is not a first-class supported mode. The resulting zap has no Nostr-graph presence (no kind 0 profile, no follows, no NIP-05) and is therefore the canonical shape that Web-of-Trust filters on relays and clients (Primal, Damus, paid relays) are built to suppress. An ephemeral-keyed zap may be technically valid but is meaningfully more likely to be filtered, downranked, or dropped than the same payment expressed as a V4V 2.0 boost. V4V 2.0's burner-key path is not subject to the same filtering surfaces because kind 30078 events do not flow through the social-trust pipeline at all — they are pulled by exact `d` tag (the payment_hash) by the recipient's bot, not surfaced in feeds.

**On reach:** zaps brought rich-comment Lightning tipping to Nostr. They created an expectation among Bitcoiners that any Lightning payment can carry a message and a sender identity. V4V 2.0 extends that same expectation to the rest of the web, so a creator's blog, a project's donation page, or a static landing site can offer the same UX a Nostr note offers — without requiring the recipient to be on a NIP-57-aware Lightning provider, and without requiring the donor to be a Nostr citizen.

## Specification

### Event: Kind 30078 Donation Boostagram

V4V 2.0 uses NIP-78 (Application-specific data) kind 30078 as the metadata carrier. Kind 30078 is parameterized-replaceable - addressable by `(kind, pubkey, d_tag)` - and is specifically designed to allow apps to store structured data without claiming a dedicated kind number.

**Required tags:**

- `["d", "<payment_hash_hex>"]` — the 64-character lowercase hex of the bolt11's payment_hash. This is the linkage to the Lightning payment. Implementations MUST NOT publish a kind 30078 unless they have successfully extracted the real payment_hash from the issued bolt11; if extraction fails, the boost MUST be aborted rather than published with a placeholder, since a non-payment-hash `d` tag produces an orphan event no recipient bot can ever match.
- `["app", "<app_name>", "<app_version>"]` — identifies the publishing client.
- `["type", "donation_boostagram"]` — distinguishes V4V 2.0 events from other kind-30078 uses.
- `["sender", "<npub or empty>"]` — the donor's npub, or the empty string for anonymous donations.
- `["recipient", "<lud16>"]` — the Lightning address of the donation recipient.
- `["amount", "<msats>"]` — the amount in millisatoshis as a stringified integer.
- `["url", "<site_url>"]` — the site root from which the boost was made. Must NOT include path or query — `https://example.com` is correct, `https://example.com/some/page?id=42` is wrong. This avoids leaking page-context information into the boost record.

**Content:** free-form text. The donor's optional written message. Empty string when no message was provided.

![Figure 2 - V4V 2.0 Kind 30078 Note Spec Proposal](https://i.nostr.build/uIfIIUziGhOIH93R.png)

**Signing:**

- *Anonymous donations:* signed by a single-use burner keypair (see "Anonymous Donations" below).
- *Attributed donations:* signed by the donor's real Nostr key, with the donor's npub in the `sender` tag (cryptographic attribution). A burner-signed-with-claimed-npub variant is permitted by the wire format but is not recommended; see "Attribution Without Sacrificing the Channel" below.

**Publishing:** the event is published to a fixed relay set (see "Relay Set" below) so bots have a known query target.

**Per-recipient publish gating (optional).** The kind 30078 sidecar exists to be read by a recipient bot. For boosts whose recipient is known not to run such a bot — e.g., a third-party Lightning address on a consumer wallet that has no dedicated boost-receipt infrastructure — implementations MAY skip the publish entirely. The Lightning payment proceeds normally; only the metadata event is omitted, on the grounds that it would have no consumer.

In split-payment deployments (multi-recipient boosts where some recipients run bots and others don't), this gating is applied per-leg: the legs whose recipients are in the implementation's bot-allowlist publish a kind 30078 each, and the legs whose recipients aren't simply pay without one. For example, in a podcast-style multi-recipient deployment, the project's own Lightning addresses might get a metadata event while guest splits and pass-through redirects pay without one.

This is a deployment-level optimization, not a protocol change. Recipients without bots receive their payment exactly as they would today; the spec's "every leg publishes a kind 30078" wording is an upper bound, not a requirement.

### Relay Set

V4V 2.0 events are published to four relays:

```
wss://relay.damus.io
wss://nos.lol
wss://relay.primal.net
wss://purplepag.es
```

These relays are chosen for durability, query reliability, and the absence of paywalls or arbitrary filtering. Recipient bots subscribe to this set when looking for boost metadata.

The fixed set is intentional. A floating "publish to the donor's relays" model would force the bot to discover the donor's NIP-65 relay list before each query adding latency and a failure mode for donors with no kind 10002. A small fixed set keeps the bot's query path simple and predictable.

### Anonymous Donations: The Burner Key

V4V 2.0 supports anonymous donation as a first-class mode, not a degraded fallback.

When the donor opts for anonymous mode, the kind 30078 event is signed by a fresh single-use Nostr keypair generated in the donor's browser at boost time. The secret key is held in memory only long enough to produce the event signature, then zeroed and discarded. The `sender` tag is left empty.

This achieves two properties simultaneously:

1. **The signature itself reveals nothing.** The event's `pubkey` field is a pseudorandom value with no on-chain history, no follow graph, no profile metadata. It's not connectable to the donor by any Nostr-native correlation.

2. **Logged-out donations work.** The donor doesn't need a Nostr identity at all. Anyone with a Lightning wallet can donate, and the metadata event still publishes — just with an entirely throwaway pubkey on the signature side.

The burner-key trick is what NIP-57 zaps cannot offer as a first-class mode: zaps require a signed kind 9734 zap-request, and while that signing key may technically be ephemeral, the resulting zap looks indistinguishable from spam to Web-of-Trust filters and is increasingly likely to be filtered or downranked by major Nostr clients and curated relays. V4V 2.0 makes anonymity the donor's choice without sacrificing the metadata channel, because the kind 30078 sidecar is pulled by exact `d` tag rather than surfaced in social feeds — there is no WoT pipeline for it to be filtered against.

### Attribution Without Sacrificing the Channel

When the donor wants attribution, the recommended mode is to sign the kind 30078 with the donor's real Nostr key. The event is then verifiably theirs: the signature itself proves that the npub in the `sender` tag (which equals the event's signing pubkey) actually authored the boost. UX cost: a signer prompt during boost generation, which in 2026 is a one-click flow for any donor with NIP-07 or NIP-46 already wired up.

A second mode is spec-permitted but **not recommended**: signing with a burner key while including the donor's npub in the `sender` tag (*claimed* attribution). This avoids the signer prompt but provides no cryptographic proof that the named donor actually authored the boost — anyone can publish such an event claiming to be from anyone. This creates an impersonation surface that V4V 2.0 implementations should generally avoid. It is documented here for completeness but should be considered a deprecated mode.

**Graceful signer-failure fallback.** Attributed-mode implementations should handle signer rejection or timeout (a common case with NIP-46 remote signers) without aborting the boost. The recommended fallback is to sign the event with a single-use burner key AND clear the `sender` tag, so the receipt degrades to anonymous mode rather than to claimed attribution. The Lightning payment still proceeds; the donor forfeits the cryptographic attribution they wanted, but the metadata event is still published and is not impersonating anyone. Falling back to claimed attribution (burner-signed while keeping the donor's npub in `sender`) is exactly the impersonation surface called out above and should be avoided.

Reference implementations expose a binary anonymous-or-not toggle: anonymous off ⇒ cryptographic attribution (real-key signed, real-npub `sender`); anonymous on ⇒ burner-key signed with empty `sender`. Claimed attribution is not exposed in the UI and is not a recommended path for new client implementations. The graceful-fallback behavior above is also implemented: if the donor selected attributed mode but the signer rejects or times out, the event is published burner-signed with an empty `sender` rather than failing the boost.

### Optional: Kind 1 Share to Feed

Donors who want their boost visible on their public Nostr feed may additionally publish a kind 1 note signed by their real Nostr key. This is independent of the kind 30078 which is explicitly for the recipient's bot; the kind 1 is for the donor's followers.

Recommended kind 1 shape:

**Content:**
```
Just boosted ⚡ <amount> sats to nostr:<recipient_npub>

<donor's optional message>

<site_url>
```

**Tags:**
- `["t", "boost"]`
- `["t", "<app_name>"]`
- `["r", "<site_url>"]`
- `["p", "<recipient_pubkey_hex>"]` — for client mention rendering and recipient notifications.
- `["client", "<app_name>"]`

**Timing:** the kind 1 should be published only after the bolt11's payment is confirmed (via LUD-21 verify polling, or LND-side observation). Publishing it before confirmation risks polluting the donor's feed with announcements for payments that never settled.

**Publish set:** the kind 1 is a regular note and is published to the donor's normal Nostr write relays — *not* the V4V 2.0 fixed relay set. The fixed set is for bot-targeted metadata only. Strict NIP-65 outbox-aware publishing (read the donor's kind 10002, fan out to that exact relay list) is the recommended mode but not required; the reference implementation currently uses its Nostr client's default publish path, which broadcasts to the connected pool. Either is acceptable so long as the kind 1 reaches the donor's followers.

## The Recipient Bot

This section describes how a reference implementation consumes the V4V 2.0 metadata stream and joins it with the recipient's Lightning payment record. The implementation is a small, single-purpose Python script that runs on a self-hosted LAN box, polls the recipient's Lightning node for new settled incoming payments, looks up matching kind 30078 events on the V4V 2.0 relay set, and publishes a kind 1 receipt note signed by the project's Nostr identity.

![Figure 3 - Bot Loop Configuration Overview](https://i.nostr.build/zORQnRMdyL01umAW.png)

### Reference implementation

The reference bot runs on an Ubuntu 24.04 mini PC on the same LAN as the recipient's Lightning node. It is a ~150-line Python 3 script with two direct runtime dependencies in the script itself (`requests` for the node's HTTP API, `websocket-client` for Nostr), plus a shared utility module that handles event signing (which pulls in `pynostr` for nsec parsing + Schnorr signing and `bech32` for npub conversion).

The Lightning node is a [Start9](https://start9.com) server running [Alby Hub](https://github.com/getAlby/hub), which is the current consumer-facing wrapper around an LDK-backed node. The bot does not talk to LND directly; Alby Hub exposes an authenticated HTTP API at `/api/transactions` that returns the same `payment_hash`, `descriptionHash`, and settlement metadata the bot would otherwise pull from `lnrpc.ListInvoices`. The abstraction is convenient: it sidesteps macaroon handling, gRPC tooling, and tor-only onion endpoints for a bearer-token HTTP request from the LAN. A bot targeting raw LND would substitute `lnrpc.SubscribeInvoices` (push) or `ListInvoices` (poll) and read `r_hash` off settled invoices; the rest of the flow is identical.

The bot's identity - the Nostr keypair it signs receipt notes with - lives in a credentials.env file intentionally outside the bot's working directory and outside any version-controlled tree. The script never reads or echoes the file directly; a small `load_config()` helper parses it into a dict at startup. The bot account is a dedicated identity, distinct from any human's personal Nostr key. 

### The boost-watch daemon

The bot is not a long-running daemon. It is a one-shot script invoked on a fixed cadence by `systemd --user`-style scheduling — a `systemd.timer` unit with `OnUnitActiveSec=10min`, which is functionally equivalent to a cron job at the same interval. Each invocation is fully self-contained: load config, fetch the latest 50 transactions from the node, filter them, query Nostr for matches, publish receipts, persist state, exit. Process lifetime is sub-second in the no-new-payments case and a few seconds when boosts are present.

State between invocations is a single ISO-8601 timestamp in a plain text file (`last_seen.txt`). Each run filters the node's transaction list down to:

- `type == "incoming"`
- `state == "settled"`
- `descriptionHash` is present (i.e. the BOLT11 commits to a hash, not a plain description — this is what the LNURL endpoint produces when issuing a V4V 2.0 invoice)
- `appId == <project_app_id>` (the integer ID Alby Hub assigns to the wallet/connection that received the payment — used to discriminate this project's incoming flow from other apps the same node serves)
- `settledAt > last_seen`

Survivors of the filter are sorted by `settledAt` ascending so multi-boost runs publish in chronological order. After successfully publishing a receipt for a boost, `last_seen` advances to that payment's `settledAt`. Failed kind 30078 lookups are the exception, handled in "Nostr query strategy" below.

The 10-minute cadence is a deliberate trade-off. Faster polling (every minute, or websocket subscriptions) would make receipts feel more real-time but would also produce noisier log output and contend with relay rate limits when no work is needed. Slower polling (hourly) lets too many boosts accumulate between runs, which dilutes the per-event "thank-you" feeling. Ten minutes is the empirical sweet spot for this implementation — also a knowing nod to Bitcoin's average block time, though there is no functional relationship between the two; the choice was driven by the polling-noise vs receipt-latency trade-offs above. Bots with a stronger real-time requirement should switch to a push model on the node side.

### Nostr query strategy

For each filtered candidate, the bot opens a websocket to each relay in the V4V 2.0 fixed publish set — `wss://relay.damus.io`, `wss://nos.lol`, `wss://relay.primal.net`, `wss://purplepag.es` — and sends a `REQ` with the filter:
```json
{ "kinds": [30078], "#d": ["<payment_hash_hex>"] }
```
Relays are queried serially, not in parallel. The first relay to return an `EVENT` short-circuits the loop: the bot closes the websocket and uses that event. If a relay sends `EOSE` with no matching event, the bot moves to the next relay. Per-relay socket timeout is 10 seconds, which covers the realistic worst case for a healthy relay; any relay that exceeds this is logged and skipped. If no relay in the set returns an event, the candidate is dropped from this run.

Querying the full publish set rather than a subset costs at most one round-trip per missing relay, but it removes the failure mode where the bot's narrow query set diverges from where the publisher actually wrote. The query relay list is imported from a shared module (`V4V_RELAYS` in `shared/boost_formatter.py`) so it stays in lockstep with the spec — a relay-set change is one edit in one place.

Implementations choose between two strategies for failed-lookup payments, picked based on the expected ratio of V4V 2.0 boosts to vanilla tips on the recipient's Lightning address:

**Strategy A — retry across runs.** When a candidate's kind 30078 isn't found on any relay in the publish set, the bot does not advance `last_seen` past that payment, so the same `payment_hash` is re-queried on the next 10-minute fire. The 10-minute cadence makes propagation races rare in practice (the 30-second propagation envelope is well below the polling interval), but the bot is forgiving in the cases that do occur. One edge case: if a *later* boost in the same run successfully publishes, the watermark advances to that later `settledAt`, and any earlier failed-lookup boost is no longer eligible for retry — this prevents the bot from getting permanently stuck on payments that will never produce a kind 30078 (direct tips to the Lightning address rather than V4V 2.0 boosts).

**Strategy B — advance and skip.** When the recipient's Lightning address receives a high volume of vanilla tips alongside V4V 2.0 boosts, retrying every vanilla tip across runs becomes wasteful — those tips will never produce a kind 30078. In this mode, the bot advances `last_seen` even on no-event lookups, accepting that a brief propagation race could cause a true V4V 2.0 boost to be missed if its event arrives after the polling fire. This is the right default when the recipient's address is widely shared (e.g., a public donation address that fields direct LN-address tips on top of V4V 2.0 boosts).

Strategy A is more forgiving for low-volume V4V-only deployments; Strategy B is more efficient for mixed-traffic deployments. The choice is per-deployment, not protocol-mandated.

### Action triggers

On a successful match, the bot does exactly one thing: publish a single kind 1 note from the project's Nostr account that announces the boost.

The note is composed deterministically from the kind 30078 event:

```
⚡ New Donation on <project name>
💰 <amount in sats, comma-formatted>
👤 nostr:<sender_npub>     (or "👤 Anon" if the sender tag is empty)
💬 "<message, with @npub/@nevent/@naddr mentions rewritten to nostr:... URIs>"
🔗 <url, suppressed if it contains 'localhost'>

#<project_tag> #Bitcoin #V4V #V4V2
```

Mention rewriting normalizes any `@npub1...`, `npub1...`, `@nevent1...`, `nevent1...`, `@naddr1...`, or `naddr1...` in the donor's free-form message to the canonical `nostr:` URI form so Nostr clients render them as proper inline mentions. The regex used has lookbehinds to skip already-prefixed entities and to skip matches glued to a preceding word character.

Beyond the formatted content, the receipt note carries NIP-57 zap split tags constructed from the boostagram's `sender` and the publishing account: equal-weight `["zap", <hex>, <relay>, "1"]` entries for each party that has a `lud16` in their kind 0 profile. With both parties present and reachable that is a 50/50 split, so any zap landing on the receipt note pays the original donor as much as the project. Anonymous boosts (empty `sender` tag) or donors without a public lud16 collapse the split to 100% to the publisher. This is a reference implementation choice, not a V4V 2.0 mandate; future implementations may pick other split structures or omit the splits entirely.

The note is built as an unsigned event tuple `[0, pubkey, created_at, kind, tags, content]`, NIP-01-serialized, hashed to produce the event `id`, and signed with the project's nsec using a Schnorr signature over the secp256k1 curve. The signed event is then broadcast to the publishing account's NIP-65 outbox: the bot reads the account's latest kind 10002 and publishes to the relays it advertises (those without a `read` marker). This is the project's own outbox, distinct from the V4V 2.0 fixed query set above — the receipt is for followers of the project's account, who will look for it on the publisher's relays. Accounts without a published kind 10002 fall back to a small hardcoded relay set for kind 1 specifically; replaceable events refuse to fall back at all, since they need to land at the author's outbox to be discoverable. Per-relay publish failures are logged but don't block the rest of the broadcast.

### Self-hosting notes

The reference setup runs on a Start9 server (Alby Hub) and a separate Ubuntu mini PC (the bot host) on the same LAN. The split is intentional: the Lightning node and its hot key sit on one purpose-built device, and the bot, which holds the project's Nostr nsec but no Lightning keys, sits on a general-purpose machine. The bot reaches the node via its `.local` LAN address and the Start9 root CA cert pinned in the bot host's trust store.

For Umbrel, RaspiBlitz, or any setup with a node-attached HTTP API, substitute the API base URL and bearer token. For raw LND without Alby Hub, swap the `requests.get(/api/transactions)` call for an `lnrpc.SubscribeInvoices` stream or `ListInvoices` paginated call against the LND gRPC interface, the rest of the script is unchanged.

## Privacy Considerations

V4V 2.0 publishes metadata to public relays. The privacy model is:

**What is exposed:**

- For anonymous boosts: amount, message, recipient, source URL, payment_hash. None of these are joinable to the donor's identity through Nostr-native correlation.
- For attributed boosts (cryptographic): all of the above, plus the donor's npub.

**What is NOT exposed:**

- Lightning routing details (who relayed the HTLC, which channels were used).
- The donor's wallet provider, unless the bolt11 itself reveals it.
- The donor's IP address, beyond what Nostr relays already see for any of their activity.

**Correlation surfaces:**

- Anyone observing Lightning gossip and querying public Nostr relays can join `payment_hash → metadata`. For attributed boosts this means they can confirm "this payment was associated with this npub."
- The `payment_hash` itself is not secret in Lightning — anyone routing the HTLC sees it. V4V 2.0 does not increase Lightning's existing exposure of `payment_hash`; it just attaches metadata to it.

**For high-privacy donations:** anonymous mode plus a Lightning wallet that doesn't bind the donor to a public identity (e.g., a fresh Phoenix wallet, or coins routed through a privacy-preserving channel) provides reasonable cover. The kind 30078 event signed by a burner key has no link to the donor through Nostr-native means.

## Why Not Just Put the Nostr Event Reference in the Lightning Comment?

A simpler-looking variant would be: include an `nevent1...` reference in the LNURL comment field (so the bolt11's description carries something like `[<app> boost] — nostr:nevent1xxx`), publish a kind 1 (or kind 30078) on Nostr with the actual metadata, and have the bot fetch it by the nevent. The Lightning comment becomes the bridge instead of `payment_hash`.

This would work, but only when the recipient controls the LNURL endpoint. If the recipient's lud16 is on a third-party service (Alby, Wallet of Satoshi, hosted LNbits), the comment lives in that service's database; the recipient's LND only sees the forwarded HTLC with no description.

The `payment_hash` approach works regardless of LNURL hosting topology: the recipient's LND always sees the payment hash on settlement. This is the architectural reason V4V 2.0 chose the Nostr-keyed-by-payment-hash model over the LNURL-comment-carries-Nostr-reference model.

If the recipient *does* control their LNURL endpoint, the simpler variant becomes available, and arguably cleaner. Future implementations may offer both modes: comment-carries-nevent for self-hosted recipients, payment-hash-keyed for everyone else. V4V 2.0 picks the universally-applicable path as the default.

## Future Work

### Cryptographically verifiable boosts: an integrity-ladder evolution path

The core V4V 2.0 design joins Lightning to Nostr by `payment_hash` alone. This is sufficient for low-stakes, high-volume tipping where forgery has no payoff, but it leaves the protocol with no native way for a third party (someone other than the recipient) to verify that a given kind 30078 corresponds to a real settled payment. NIP-57 zaps achieve this third-party-verifiability by having the recipient's LNURL provider cryptographically attest to settlement via the kind 9735 receipt. V4V 2.0 cannot adopt that mechanism without giving up its any-LNURL property.

The path described here approaches comparable trust properties without that sacrifice, by adding three composable mechanisms — a bolt11 commitment tag, a post-settlement preimage event, and a chained kind 1 receipt — that together let third parties verify the entire payment-to-metadata chain end to end. Each layer can be adopted independently; together they bring V4V 2.0's integrity guarantees into the same neighborhood as zaps.

#### Layer 1 — bolt11 commitment tag

The donor includes the full bolt11 invoice they intend to pay as a tag on the kind 30078:

```
["bolt11", "<bolt11_string>"]
```

The recipient's bot verifies, on lookup: the bolt11 in the tag exists in its own settled-payments table, and the payment_hash extracted from that bolt11 matches the event's `d` tag. This closes the forgery vector where an attacker observing Lightning gossip sees a payment_hash and writes a fake kind 30078 around it — that attacker doesn't have the bolt11 string, only the recipient's LNURL endpoint and the payer ever did. The bar for forgery rises from "passive observer of Lightning" to "active interceptor of the LNURL response or the donor's wallet flow."

It does not provide cryptographic binding from the invoice to the metadata; that requires bolt11's `description_hash` field, which can only be set by the LNURL endpoint at issuance time (and is the next-tier evolution path documented separately below).

#### Layer 2 — post-settlement preimage event

The Lightning preimage — the 32 bytes whose SHA-256 is the payment_hash — is known only to the payer (and, on settlement, to the recipient). Anyone holding a valid preimage either paid the invoice or is the recipient; an attacker observing Lightning gossip cannot derive it.

After payment settles, the donor's client publishes a second kind 30078 with the same `d` tag (the payment_hash), this time including:

```
["preimage", "<32-byte-hex>"]
```

Because kind 30078 is parameterized-replaceable, the second event replaces the first on relays — provided both events are signed by the same pubkey. In anonymous mode this means the burner key MUST persist in client memory across the payment window (typically 2–4 seconds with single-hop addresses) rather than being zeroed immediately after the first publish; once the second event is signed and published, the burner key is zeroed.

The bot verifies on lookup that `sha256(preimage) == payment_hash` from the `d` tag. This is cryptographic proof that the publisher actually paid the invoice (or holds the recipient's settlement records, which only the recipient does).

This UX cost is small in deployments that already keep the donor on-page through settlement (banner, page-leave guards, LUD-21 verify polling). The window between payment and second publish is a few hundred milliseconds; the donor is staying on the page anyway.

**Graceful degradation.** The preimage event may fail to publish (browser closes, network drops, relay flake). Bots SHOULD adopt the following fallback: if a preimage event is absent at lookup time but a Layer 1 bolt11 tag is present and validates, accept the boost as Layer-1-verified. This avoids punishing donors for an event whose absence has no consequence on the Lightning side — the payment already settled. Stricter deployments may instead refuse to publish receipts without a preimage; this is a per-bot policy, not a protocol mandate.

**Wallet support note.** The donor's client extracts the preimage from one of: WebLN's `sendPayment` response, NWC's `pay_invoice` response, or the LUD-21 verify endpoint's settlement response. Wallets that do not expose the preimage to the donor's client cannot participate in Layer 2 on the donor side — but the bot can still ask the recipient's node for the preimage on settlement and treat the boost as Layer-2-verified post-hoc.

#### Layer 3 — chained kind 1 receipt published by the recipient

The reference implementation already publishes a kind 1 receipt note from the project's Nostr account on each successful boost. With Layers 1 and 2 in place, that receipt can carry the full verification chain so that any third party — not just the recipient's bot — can audit the boost end to end:

```
kind: 1
content: "⚡ New Donation on <project> ..."
tags:
  ["e", "<30078_event_id>"],          # points at the metadata event
  ["b", "<payment_hash>"],            # single-letter tag for indexing
  ["preimage", "<hex>"],              # cryptographic settlement proof
  ["bolt11", "<bolt11_string>"],      # full invoice for verification
  ["amount", "<msats>"],
  ["sender", "<npub_or_empty>"],
  ["app", "<app_name>", "<version>"],
  ["t", "boost"],
  ...zap split tags as in the current reference impl...
```

Any third party scrolling the project's feed can now verify, without trusting the project:

1. Fetch the referenced kind 30078 by event id; verify its signature (proves *some* publisher authored the metadata).
2. Verify `sha256(preimage) == payment_hash`, where `payment_hash` is decoded from the bolt11 (proves the invoice was actually paid).
3. Decode the bolt11 and verify its amount matches the receipt's `amount` tag (proves the payment was for the claimed sum).
4. Optionally verify the bolt11's payee node corresponds to the recipient's lightning address node (defense against a malicious receipt-publisher forging recipient claims).

The project's account is still vouching that "this 30078 belongs to this payment" — Layer 1 strengthens that claim but doesn't fully cryptographically bind it without `description_hash`. Everything else in the chain is independently verifiable by anyone with relay and Lightning gossip access.

#### What this gets us, and how close it lands to NIP-57

With all three layers active, V4V 2.0 achieves third-party-verifiable settlement attestation with a single-trusted-party chain (the recipient's receipt-publishing account) of comparable cardinality to NIP-57's (the recipient's LNURL provider). The remaining gaps relative to NIP-57:

- **Cryptographic invoice-to-metadata binding.** NIP-57's `description_hash = sha256(zap_request)` ensures the invoice itself commits to the comment. Layer 1 only proves the publisher knew the bolt11. The next-tier evolution path (description_hash binding, see below) closes this gap fully but requires recipient-controlled LNURL.
- **Automatic receipt issuance.** NIP-57 receipts come for free from the LNURL provider for every paid zap. V4V 2.0 receipts only exist if the recipient runs a bot. This is a deployment difference rather than a cryptographic one — equivalent integrity, different operational model.

Within those bounds, this evolution path is the cleanest way to bring NIP-57-style trust properties to deployments where standard zaps don't fit. It does not require recipient-controlled LNURL (Layers 1–2 work with any LNURL), and the third-party-verifiability of Layer 3 only requires the recipient to run a single-purpose bot — the same architectural footprint the reference implementation already has.

#### Bringing zaps to the open web — and to podcasting

Zaps mainstreamed the expectation that any Lightning payment can carry a signed comment, an attributed identity, and a third-party-verifiable settlement record. That expectation has been confined to Nostr because zaps require Nostr-native infrastructure on both sides of the payment. With the integrity-ladder layers in place, V4V 2.0 extends the same expectation to:

**Web destinations.** A creator's blog, a project's donation page, or a static landing site can offer zap-equivalent UX (rich comments, attributed senders, verifiable settlement) using only standard LNURL on the recipient side and any Lightning wallet on the donor side. The kind 1 receipt becomes the public artifact that other Nostr clients can display, so a boost on a website surfaces in the recipient's Nostr feed with the same fidelity as a zap on a note. This brings the social-proof and discovery surface of zaps to content that lives outside Nostr.

**Podcasting.** The Podcasting 2.0 boostagram protocol gave podcast listeners rich tipping but only on keysend-aware sender wallets, which constrained the audience to a small set of purpose-built apps. A podcasting client implementing V4V 2.0 (with the Layer 1–3 integrity ladder) could let listeners boost an episode from any Lightning wallet, while the podcaster's bot publishes verifiable kind 1 receipts that surface in Nostr feeds as "this listener boosted this episode." Two pieces are required: a podcasting client that implements the V4V 2.0 publish-side flow (kind 30078 with episode metadata in tags, plus the preimage-event step), and a recipient-side bot that listens for settled payments on the podcaster's node and issues the chained kind 1 receipt. Neither piece is large; the podcasting client work is mostly UX (episode-aware boost composer) layered over the same publish flow a web boost uses, and the recipient bot is a near-duplicate of the reference implementation. Once both exist, podcast boosts gain Nostr-native discoverability and per-boost cryptographic auditability — properties keysend boostagrams never had.

In both cases, the integrity ladder is what makes the comparison to NIP-57 meaningful rather than aspirational. Without these layers, V4V 2.0 is a pragmatic metadata sidecar with weaker integrity than zaps. With them, it is a full peer mechanism for the segments of the internet where zaps cannot natively reach.

### Backend-issued invoices with bidirectional `description_hash` linking

The most spec-clean evolution would be a recipient-controlled invoice-issuing backend that bakes a Nostr event reference into the bolt11's `description_hash`. Specifically: the backend computes `description_hash = sha256(canonical_form_of(kind_30078_event))` and includes that in the issued invoice. The bolt11 then carries a verifiable cryptographic reference to the metadata event.

This eliminates the asymmetric "donor publishes first, bot looks up later" model in favor of a fully cryptographic bidirectional link. It requires the recipient to control the LNURL endpoint, which not all recipients do. V4V 2.0 leaves this as an opt-in evolution path; the static-site default remains the `payment_hash`-keyed sidecar.

### bolt12 offers

bolt12 is the long-term protocol successor to bolt11 and LNURL-pay. It includes structured metadata fields natively and removes many of the limitations LUD-12 comments impose. As wallet support for bolt12 matures, a future V4V version may target bolt12 directly and obsolete the Nostr sidecar entirely. This is years away.

### Spec consolidation

The "V4V 2.0" naming acknowledges the conceptual descent from Podcasting 2.0's boostagram model while signaling the architectural break (LNURL+Nostr versus keysend+TLV). If the protocol gains adoption beyond this implementation, formalizing it as a Nostr Improvement Proposal (NIP) with a dedicated kind range would replace the kind-30078-with-namespacing-tags approach.

## Open Questions and Limitations

- **No bidirectional cryptographic link in the base protocol.** The bolt11 has no awareness of the kind 30078 event in the Layer 0 (current default) configuration. A bad actor could forge a kind 30078 with a correct `payment_hash` and false metadata, hoping a bot displays it. Bots should validate that the `recipient` tag matches their own lud16 before trusting any other field. The integrity-ladder evolution path documented in Future Work raises this bar substantially through the bolt11 commitment tag and post-settlement preimage event; full cryptographic bidirectional binding is achievable via the `description_hash` evolution path for recipients controlling their own LNURL endpoint.

- **`payment_hash` on a public relay.** As noted in the privacy section, this creates a small public correlation surface. Acceptable trade-off for the discovery benefits.

- **Custodial-wallet recipients get no benefit.** Recipients who use Strike, Wallet of Satoshi, or other custodial services do not have access to their underlying Lightning records, including the `payment_hash`. For them, V4V 2.0 metadata exists on Nostr but is operationally inaccessible. They get the same UX as today, "received N sats" with no context. V4V 2.0 doesn't worsen their position; it just doesn't help.

- **LNURL endpoint variability.** Some LNURL-pay servers may not advertise the fields we expect, may impose unusual comment limits, or may modify the description in unexpected ways. V4V 2.0 implementations should degrade gracefully when LNURL metadata is unusual.

- **Race condition on instant payments.** The donor publishes the kind 30078 before paying, but instant Lightning payments may settle before the metadata propagates across the four-relay set. Bot implementations should retry the Nostr query a few times before treating the absence of metadata as definitive. A short retry window (e.g. 2-5 seconds between attempts, up to 30 seconds total) handles realistic propagation delays.
